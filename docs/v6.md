_Last updated: 2026-04-09_

---

## 1. Overview

Oblixa V6 should be the version where the platform becomes a **continuous contract assurance and adaptive operations system**.

V4 made signed contracts executable.  
V5 made contract operations controllable at the portfolio level through decisions, campaigns, simulations, relationship workspaces, limited external collaboration, and control-room UX.

V6 should go one step further:
- continuously monitor portfolio health,
- detect operational drift before failure,
- evaluate contracts against machine-readable control policies,
- launch governed playbooks automatically or semi-automatically,
- measure whether interventions actually improved outcomes,
- create a closed-loop operating system for post-signature contract assurance.

### V6 one-line definition

**Oblixa V6 is the contract assurance system that continuously detects risk, applies governed playbooks, and closes the loop between policy, execution, and outcomes.**

---

## 2. What V6 should accomplish

V6 should make Oblixa not just a place where work is tracked and decisions are made, but a system that actively keeps a portfolio healthy over time.

The product should answer questions like:
- Where is operational drift emerging before it becomes visible as failure?
- Which controls are weak, stale, or being bypassed?
- Which teams, counterparties, or programs are repeatedly producing avoidable issues?
- Which policy changes improved performance, and which only created more work?
- Which low-risk actions can the system safely trigger automatically?
- What portfolio segments require proactive attention this week, not just after a deadline is missed?

This is the shift from:
- **control and coordination**
to
- **continuous assurance and adaptive execution**

---

## 3. Strategic thesis

### Core thesis

V6 turns Oblixa from a portfolio control plane into a **closed-loop contract assurance platform**.

### Why this is the right next step

V5 already provides:
- decision orchestration,
- campaigns,
- simulations,
- intelligence and capacity signals,
- relationship workspaces,
- external action flows,
- control-room UX.

That is a strong control layer. The next source of value is not more ways to manually manage work. It is the ability to:
1. detect issues earlier,
2. trigger the right intervention with less human coordination,
3. measure whether the intervention worked,
4. continuously improve programs, policies, and operational controls.

In other words, V6 should make Oblixa an **adaptive system**, not just a structured one.

## 4. Product definition

## What Oblixa V6 is

Oblixa V6 is a platform for:
- continuous contract operations monitoring,
- control-policy evaluation,
- governed playbook execution,
- portfolio health scoring and drift detection,
- safe operational autopilot for bounded actions,
- learning from outcomes over time,
- producing assurance-grade evidence for managers and reviewers.

## What Oblixa V6 is not

V6 should still avoid becoming:
- a full pre-signature CLM suite,
- a generic AI operations platform,
- a full enterprise GRC suite,
- a procurement/vendor-spend platform,
- a project-management replacement,
- an unconstrained autonomous-agent product.

The scope remains post-signature contract operations, but with deeper intelligence and assurance.

## 5. Product posture after V5

After V5, Oblixa can:
- execute work,
- coordinate decisions,
- run campaigns,
- simulate changes,
- support external actions,
- expose portfolio state.

V6 should add three higher-order capabilities:

1. **Assurance**  
   Continuous control and drift monitoring instead of periodic review alone.

2. **Adaptive intervention**  
   Trigger the right playbook, escalation, campaign, or decision path based on live signals.

3. **Outcome learning**  
   Measure whether policies, programs, campaigns, and recommendations improved the portfolio.

## 7. V6 design principles

### 1. Detection before failure
The system should surface weak signals before they become missed deadlines or escalations.

### 2. Controls must be machine-readable
Policies and expectations should be explicit enough to evaluate automatically.

### 3. Automation must remain governed
Automation should be bounded, visible, reversible where feasible, and policy-constrained.

### 4. Health matters more than activity
The product should optimize for portfolio health, not only workflow volume.

### 5. Explanations are mandatory
Every alert, score, recommendation, or automated action must show the evidence and rule path behind it.

### 6. Improvement must be measurable
The product should not only act; it should measure whether the action improved outcomes.

## 9. Core V6 feature set

## 9.1 Control Policies 2.0

### Summary
Expand policy from configuration into an explicit control system with evaluation rules, control objectives, enforcement modes, and evidence expectations.

### Control policy concepts
A control policy should define:
- objective,
- scope,
- applicable contract/program/account segments,
- required work objects,
- required evidence state,
- SLA or timing thresholds,
- severity model,
- exemption rules,
- enforcement mode,
- remediation playbook binding.

### Enforcement modes
- observe only
- warn
- create exception
- require decision workspace
- trigger campaign
- trigger autopilot action
- escalate immediately

### Example policies
- “All enterprise renewals within 60 days require finance + legal review.”
- “All annual obligations above sensitivity threshold require fresh evidence within 30 days.”
- “No contract in this segment may remain ownerless for more than 3 business days.”
- “Decision packets of type renewal must include approved pricing rationale.”

### Why it matters
This makes Oblixa a control system rather than just a workflow system.

---

## 9.2 Continuous Assurance Checks

### Assurance domains

- control-policy compliance,

### Output types

- control pass/fail state,
- “watch” signals,

### Behaviors
- recurring scheduled evaluation,
- event-triggered reevaluation after important changes,
- per-segment rollups,
- trend and delta capture.

## 9.3 Adaptive Playbooks

### Summary
Introduce a playbook system that links signals and controls to concrete operational interventions.

### Playbook types
- create decision workspace,
- start campaign,
- escalate manager,
- assign backup owner,
- request evidence refresh,
- reopen exception,
- trigger stakeholder review,
- generate packet,
- send bounded external request,
- schedule focused report pack,
- route contracts into maintenance campaign.

### Playbook stages
- eligibility,
- preconditions,
- dry run / preview,
- approval requirement,
- execution,
- follow-up checks,
- success/failure assessment,
- postmortem summary.

### Why it matters
A healthy control system does not stop at detecting issues. It needs a repeatable intervention model.

---

## 9.4 Safe Autopilot

### Summary
Allow Oblixa to take a carefully bounded set of low-risk actions automatically under policy control.

### Autopilot-suitable actions
- assign reminder recipients,
- issue evidence refresh requests,
- open a standard decision workspace,
- attach a default playbook or program,
- add a contract to a campaign,
- request missing stakeholder input,
- send follow-up nudges,
- generate review packets,
- schedule report pack delivery.

### Guardrails
- explicit per-org enablement,
- per-action allowlist,
- severity and sensitivity thresholds,
- dry-run history,
- human approval for higher-risk actions,
- reversal support where safe,
- full event logging.

### Why it matters
This is how Oblixa becomes truly operationally leveraged without becoming recklessly autonomous.

## 9.6 Assurance Findings and Findings Queue

### Summary
Create a formal findings model above exceptions.

### Difference from exceptions
Exceptions are operational breakdowns tied to workflow objects.  
Findings are assurance-level observations tied to controls, patterns, or repeated behavior.

### Finding examples
- evidence freshness degrading across one segment,
- approval SLA drift emerging in finance reviews,
- repeated external response failures by one counterparty,
- rising owner churn in one portfolio cluster,
- mismatch between policy intent and actual execution.

### Features
- severity,
- confidence,
- scope,
- linked controls,
- linked entities,
- recommended playbook,
- analyst note,
- resolution status,
- audit trail.

### Why it matters
This elevates the platform from workflow management to operational assurance.

## 9.7 Outcome Intelligence

### Summary
Measure whether interventions actually improved outcomes.

### Questions answered
- Did this campaign reduce exception recurrence?
- Did this policy change improve SLA performance?
- Did this playbook shorten time-to-resolution?
- Did this recommendation improve renewal readiness?
- Which program versions create cleaner execution?
- Which external flows have the best response yield?

### Metrics produced
- before/after comparison,
- intervention effectiveness score,
- recurrence reduction,
- time-to-stability,
- workload tradeoff,
- false-positive/false-negative rates,
- recommendation effectiveness.

### Why it matters
Without this, the platform only creates more process. With it, the platform learns.

## 10. New major workflows

## Workflow 1: Assurance finding to intervention
1. Continuous assurance detects a weakening pattern.
2. System opens a finding.
3. A recommended playbook is attached.
4. Manager reviews or autopilot preconditions are checked.
5. Playbook runs.
6. Follow-up checks measure whether health improved.
7. Outcome is logged for future effectiveness scoring.

## Workflow 2: Policy breach with adaptive remediation
1. Control policy evaluator detects a breach across a segment.
2. System calculates scope and severity.
3. Simulation previews candidate actions.
4. A campaign or decision workspace is created.
5. Stakeholders execute remediation.
6. Assurance checks verify restoration to healthy state.

## Workflow 3: External evidence refresh loop
1. Evidence freshness falls below threshold.
2. System issues external refresh request.
3. External participant submits update or misses deadline.
4. Review and validation occur.
5. Failure escalates to finding or exception.
6. Control objective re-evaluates after response.

## Workflow 4: Program performance tuning
1. Outcome intelligence shows one program version underperforming.
2. Program Evolution Studio proposes changes.
3. Change is simulated on a target segment.
4. Staged rollout begins.
5. Assurance scorecards track results.
6. Successful version expands.

## Workflow 5: Portfolio board review
1. Weekly board packet is generated.
2. Findings, scorecards, decision queues, and campaign drift are summarized.
3. Board decisions create follow-up work and control changes.
4. Subsequent board compares effect versus previous period.

## 19. Risks and failure modes

### Risk 1: Too much abstraction
If assurance becomes too theoretical, users will not act on it.  
**Mitigation:** every finding must map to real objects and a concrete next action.

### Risk 2: Autopilot overreach
Automation that feels unsafe or opaque will damage trust.  
**Mitigation:** start with low-risk actions, strong explainability, and visible controls.

### Risk 3: Score inflation without signal quality
Poor findings or noisy scores will make the system feel arbitrary.  
**Mitigation:** calibrate with outcome feedback and show drivers transparently.

### Risk 4: Excessive operational complexity
More controls, boards, and playbooks can become burdensome.  
**Mitigation:** ship strong defaults and role-based views; avoid forcing configuration depth on all users.

### Risk 5: Drifting into generic GRC
That would blur the product and reduce focus.  
**Mitigation:** keep every assurance object tied to signed-contract operations.

### Risk 6: Weak evidence of improvement
If the platform cannot show intervention impact, assurance loses value.  
**Mitigation:** build outcome intelligence early, not as a late add-on.

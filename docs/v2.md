**ContractOps V2**

Functional Expansion Plan for a Professional-Grade Contract Operations Platform

|**Baseline**|V1 as currently built and fully functional|
| :- | :- |
|**Document purpose**|Define the next-version feature expansion that materially increases professional workflow value and demonstrated impact|
|**Planning posture**|Use the as-built spec as the source of truth; treat V2 as an expansion layer, not a rebuild|

|<p>**Working thesis**</p><p>V1 already handles centralization, extraction, review, reminders, organizations, billing, export, and extraction jobs. V2 should therefore focus on becoming a deeper operating system for professionals: more workflow ownership, more repeatable actions, more reporting, more collaboration, and more integration into everyday contract work.</p>|
| :- |



**1. Executive summary**

ContractOps should use V2 to move from a capable contract tracker into a more embedded contract operations workspace. The strongest path is not broad legal-tech ambition. It is deeper usefulness inside the workflows professionals already run: intake, ownership, review, follow-up, reporting, renewal planning, and cross-team accountability.

Because V1 is already fully functional, V2 should be framed as an expansion of workflow depth. The goal is for a professional user to rely on the product not only to store agreements and receive reminders, but to actively run recurring operational work from it.

|<p>**V2 outcome standard**</p><p>A professional team should be able to say that ContractOps is where they see what matters, assign what needs action, track what changed, prepare for upcoming renewals, and prove what happened.</p>|
| :- |

**2. V1 baseline and what it already enables**

The current product already provides the foundation for a serious operations tool. It includes organization-scoped workspaces, roles, contract records, file storage, AI-assisted field extraction, tracked extraction jobs, dashboards, review workflows, reminders, export, Stripe-backed billing, and supporting observability and deployment primitives.

|**V1 layer**|**Already present**|**Implication for V2**|
| :-: | :-: | :-: |
|**Core record system**|Contracts, files, extracted fields, reminders, audit events|V2 can deepen workflows around the contract record instead of first solving basic storage|
|**Operational processing**|Tracked extraction jobs, review surface, reminder cron, CSV export|V2 can emphasize actionability, reporting, and repeatable work|
|**Team model**|Organizations, roles, invites, settings, billing|V2 can safely add accountability, collaboration, and workspace controls|
|**Platform readiness**|Sentry support, CI, Vercel-oriented deployment, API routes|V2 can add higher-value capabilities without changing the architectural center|

This baseline matters strategically. V2 should not spend its energy re-solving contract upload, extraction, or billing. It should spend its energy turning those primitives into a professional workflow system with visibly higher day-to-day value.

**3. V2 product objective**

The objective of V2 is to make ContractOps useful enough that a professional team would keep it open throughout the month, not only when uploading a contract or receiving a reminder.

The practical standard is simple: the product should become meaningfully involved in at least five recurring jobs professionals already perform around client agreements.

**•** Triage newly signed or newly received agreements and confirm who owns follow-up.

**•** Review upcoming dates and decide what requires action this week and this month.

**•** Track non-date obligations, missing information, and unresolved questions against each contract.

**•** Prepare renewals, notice actions, and internal approvals from one place.

**•** Report contract status and upcoming exposure to leadership, finance, or client-facing owners.



**4. Design principles for V2**

**1.** Workflow depth over feature breadth. Every major addition should slot into a recurring professional workflow. New capabilities should not feel impressive but disconnected.

**2.** Actionability over passive visibility. Dashboards are useful, but V2 should convert visibility into assignments, queues, decisions, and completed work.

**3.** Trust-preserving automation. Automation should speed work without weakening confidence. Humans should be able to see why a field, task, or rule exists and what triggered it.

**4.** Configuration without platform sprawl. Professionals need some flexibility, but V2 should avoid turning into an abstract, fully custom system too early.

**5.** Evidence of impact. Each feature should be evaluable in operational terms such as time saved, fewer misses, faster reviews, cleaner handoffs, or stronger reporting.

**5. Target professional workflows to own**

V2 should expand into a defined set of operating workflows. This keeps the version coherent and makes product value easier to demonstrate.

|**Workflow**|**Current V1 support**|**V2 expansion**|**User value**|
| :-: | :-: | :-: | :-: |
|**Intake**|Upload, extraction, review|Bulk intake, structured queueing, ownership assignment, intake status|Faster time from agreement receipt to usable record|
|**Ongoing management**|Dashboard, reminders, search|Tasking, watchlists, contract health, monthly review views|The app becomes an operating console|
|**Renewal prep**|Date tracking and reminders|Renewal workspace, scenario notes, approval checkpoints, playbooks|Teams can prepare instead of only react|
|**Cross-team handoff**|Roles and org membership|Mentions, requests, comments, ownership transitions, approvals|Less work lost between legal, ops, and finance|
|**Reporting**|CSV export and dashboard stats|Saved reports, exceptions views, stakeholder summaries, trend metrics|Leaders see exposure and progress quickly|

|<p>**Interpretation**</p><p>The central V2 shift is from record-centric utility to workflow-centric utility. Professionals should feel that the system helps them coordinate work, not merely store metadata.</p>|

**6. V2 capability pillars**

**6.1 Workflow control and operational status**

V1 has contract records and statuses, but V2 should introduce richer operating states that reflect what professionals actually need to manage. Each contract should have a visible operational lifecycle beyond "exists in the system."

**•** Intake status such as awaiting review, in clarification, active, at risk, renewal prep, notice decision, archived.

**•** Contract health indicators that combine missing fields, unresolved obligations, stale ownership, and upcoming deadlines.

**•** Required-next-step logic so each contract clearly shows what should happen next and who should do it.

**•** Portfolio views grouped by operating status rather than only by database fields.

**6.2 Tasking, follow-up, and accountability**

Professionals need to turn contract facts into work. V2 should add a lightweight task system tied directly to contracts, fields, reminders, and events.

**•** Tasks created manually or from rules, with assignee, due date, status, priority, and linked contract context.

**•** Ability to request clarification from an owner or teammate on a specific field, obligation, or renewal decision.

**•** Open-work queues by person, by team, and by contract portfolio.

**•** Automatic carry-forward of tasks when ownership changes, with a visible audit trail of the transition.

**6.3 Renewal and notice planning workspace**

The current reminder model is useful, but professionals often need a richer process leading up to a renewal or notice window.

**•** Dedicated renewal-prep views that pull together the contract, upcoming dates, owner, task list, notes, and decision checkpoints.

**•** Configurable lead-time playbooks such as 120/90/60/30-day actions before a renewal or notice deadline.

**•** Scenario tracking such as renew, renegotiate, terminate, extend temporarily, or awaiting leadership decision.

**•** Internal approval tracking so important commercial decisions do not live in email or memory.



**6. V2 capability pillars (continued)**

**6.4 Obligations and operating commitments**

Date tracking is only part of professional contract work. V2 should introduce a narrow but meaningful obligations layer so teams can track non-date commitments that affect delivery and compliance.

**•** Obligation records linked to a contract, such as reporting duties, notice requirements, invoice dependencies, insurance obligations, or deliverable checkpoints.

**•** Owner, due date or cadence, status, and evidence notes for each obligation.

**•** Recurring obligation templates for common contract types.

**•** Obligations surfaced on the dashboard alongside pure date-based reminders.

**6.5 Notes, commentary, and operational memory**

Professionals need lightweight context around the record. V2 should provide structured notes and commentary without trying to become a general chat platform.

**•** Contract timeline notes attached to significant events such as review completion, owner change, renewal decision, or escalation.

**•** Field-level comments so users can capture why a value was changed, left unresolved, or approved with caveats.

**•** Pinned operational notes for the next reviewer or owner.

**•** Mentions and notifications for time-sensitive internal coordination.

**6.6 Reporting, saved views, and executive summaries**

A professional tool becomes more valuable when it can answer recurring management questions quickly. V2 should move beyond static dashboard tiles into repeatable reporting.

**•** Saved filtered views by region, owner, contract type, renewal month, at-risk status, or missing data.

**•** Scheduled internal summary emails for leadership or operations owners.

**•** Exception reports such as contracts with missing notice logic, no owner, unresolved extraction fields, or upcoming decisions without tasks.

**•** Trends over time such as review completion velocity, contracts by status, and renewal concentration by month.

**6.7 Intake, migration, and portfolio maintenance**

For professional utility, the product must reduce operational friction at scale. V2 should improve how teams get large bodies of work into the system and keep them current.

**•** Bulk intake with progress tracking, structured validation, and clear error states.

**•** Spreadsheet-assisted import for legacy portfolios where direct file ingestion is incomplete.

**•** Change detection and re-extraction workflows when a newer contract file supersedes an earlier one.

**•** Maintenance views for stale records, orphaned files, duplicate entries, and contracts with missing required fields.

**6.8 Integrations and outward usefulness**

Professionals rarely work in a single tool. V2 should add a modest but valuable integration layer that increases relevance without creating an enterprise integration burden too early.

**•** Calendar export or sync for key dates.

**•** Webhook or API endpoints for contract events and reminder events.

**•** Slack or email-to-task patterns for notifications and follow-up.

**•** CRM or client-system linking through external IDs rather than full bidirectional system complexity.

**8. Detailed workflow expansions**

**8.1 Intake-to-live workflow**

V2 should make the path from new agreement to trusted operating record substantially faster and more explicit.

**•** A contract enters an intake queue with owner, source, ingestion state, and extraction state visible together.

**•** Reviewers can resolve extraction issues in a dedicated queue rather than through scattered navigation.

**•** Required data checks prevent a contract from being treated as operationally active until minimum fields, owner, and key dates are confirmed.

**•** The system records the transition from received to reviewed to active so operations teams can measure throughput.

**8.2 Renewal management workflow**

V2 should treat renewal preparation as a workflow, not just a date. This is one of the clearest opportunities to become highly valuable to professionals.

**•** A renewal queue groups contracts entering a lead-time window and orders them by urgency and value.

**•** Each contract shows a recommended playbook: review pricing, confirm client status, collect internal decision, prepare notice if needed, finalize disposition.

**•** Owners can log interim decisions and blockers so status is visible before the deadline becomes urgent.

**•** Leadership or finance stakeholders can see a summary view of renewal exposure for the next one to two quarters.

**8.3 Ownership and handoff workflow**

Professional teams lose value when agreement knowledge sits with one person. V2 should formalize handoffs.

**•** Every contract should have a clear primary owner and optional secondary stakeholders.

**•** Ownership change should cascade intelligently across reminders, open tasks, pending approvals, and watchlists.

**•** A handoff note or checklist should preserve context for the next owner.

**•** Management views should surface contracts that have gone ownerless or have stale ownership.

**8.4 Reporting and review cadence workflow**

V2 should support recurring management rituals such as weekly operations reviews and monthly exposure reviews.

**•** A weekly review mode highlights new intake, unresolved exceptions, deadlines inside a configurable horizon, and contracts needing ownership clarification.

**•** A monthly portfolio summary provides counts, key upcoming dates, at-risk records, and contracts awaiting renewal decisions.

**•** Saved review packets should support quick export or distribution to stakeholders.

**•** The reporting layer should make the product useful even to leaders who do not edit records directly.

**9. Information model additions required for V2**

To support these workflows cleanly, V2 should expand the data model in a deliberate way rather than forcing everything into the existing contract and reminder tables.

|**Entity**|**Purpose**|**Notes**|
| :-: | :-: | :-: |
|**Contract tasks**|Represent action items linked to contracts, obligations, or renewal events|Separate from reminders; supports assignee, priority, status, due date, and audit history|
|**Obligations**|Track non-date commitments and recurring duties|Can support templates by contract type later|
|**Comments / notes**|Store operational context at contract or field level|Should distinguish pinned notes from timeline comments|
|**Saved views**|Persist filter logic and reporting presets|Useful for role-based or team-based recurring reviews|
|**Playbooks / templates**|Reusable rules for reminders, tasks, obligations, and review logic|Keep scoped and opinionated in V2|
|**Approval records**|Track internal decisions on renewal or notice actions|Can start lightweight before formal workflow expansion|

|<p>**Constraint**</p><p>The model should stay grounded in contract operations. V2 should resist turning every workflow into a free-form project-management system.</p>|

**10. User roles and persona expansion**

V1 already has role-based access. V2 should expand product usefulness by acknowledging the distinct jobs different professionals perform.

**•** Operations lead: needs portfolio visibility, queues, exceptions, and confidence that nothing important is unowned.

**•** Account or client owner: needs upcoming actions, renewal preparation, and concise context on their contracts.

**•** Finance or revenue operations: needs renewal concentration, payment-related references, and upcoming decisions that affect revenue planning.

**•** Founder or manager: needs summary reporting, risks, and quick access to the contracts most likely to require intervention.

**•** Reviewer or contract coordinator: needs high-throughput review tools, missing-data queues, and clean task routing.



**11. What V2 should deliberately avoid**

A useful professional product still needs boundaries. The following areas should be resisted unless direct user evidence forces them into scope.

**•** Full contract lifecycle management breadth such as redlining, negotiation workrooms, or external counterparty collaboration.

**•** Abstract custom-object flexibility that makes the product behave like a generic workflow builder.

**•** Heavy clause-analysis ambition that competes with specialized legal AI products.

**•** Complex enterprise integration programs before the core operating workflows prove strong standalone value.

**•** Too many configuration switches that make the product harder to understand or support.

**13. Demonstrated-impact metrics for V2**

Because the purpose of V2 is demonstrated professional impact, metrics should be framed around workflow adoption and operational outcomes rather than vanity usage alone.

|**Metric category**|**What to measure**|**Why it matters**|
| :-: | :-: | :-: |
|**Activation**|Time from upload to trusted active contract; percentage of contracts with owner + key dates + status|Shows whether the system becomes usable quickly|
|**Workflow adoption**|Tasks created per active workspace; percentage of renewals managed through the renewal workspace|Shows whether users rely on V2 workflows|
|**Portfolio hygiene**|Contracts missing owners, missing fields, or stale statuses|Indicates whether the product improves operating discipline|
|**Operational execution**|Completion rate for pre-deadline tasks and obligations; overdue-task rate|Reflects real follow-through|
|**Management usefulness**|Use of saved views, summary reports, and monthly review exports|Shows value beyond frontline editors|
|**Retention signal**|Percentage of active workspaces returning weekly or monthly to review queues and upcoming actions|Demonstrates embeddedness in real work|

|<p>**Impact test**</p><p>If users only upload contracts and occasionally view reminders, V2 has not gone far enough. If they review tasks weekly, use renewal workspaces, depend on saved views, and route ownership through the product, the product has become materially more valuable.</p>|
| :- |

**14. Final recommendation**

V2 should be defined as a workflow-expansion release. The product already has enough foundational capability to support a more ambitious operating layer. The best next move is to deepen professional usefulness through tasks, obligations, renewal planning, reporting, collaboration, and integrations that reinforce recurring work.

The most important discipline is to keep V2 coherent. Every major feature should answer a practical question: does this help a professional team run contract operations more reliably, with more visibility and less coordination loss?

If the answer is yes across multiple recurring workflows, ContractOps will not merely look complete. It will feel necessary.

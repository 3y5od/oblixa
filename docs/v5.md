_Last updated: 2026-04-09_

---

## 1. Overview

Oblixa V5 should not be “more modules.”

It should be the version where Oblixa evolves from a **contract execution platform** into a **contract control and decision system** for professionals running recurring, high-stakes post-signature workflows.

V4 established the execution foundation:
- contract programs,
- execution graph,
- exceptions,
- evidence and attestations,
- report packs,
- policy simulation,
- role-aware command surfaces,
- cron-driven operational workflows.

That is already a substantial platform. V5 should build on that by helping teams answer a harder class of questions:

- Which contracts are exposed to the same risk or change?
- Which renewals or obligations require a coordinated decision, not just a task?
- How do we roll out a new policy or commercial change across a portfolio?
- How do we involve internal and external stakeholders without losing control?
- How do we explain, forecast, and prove the operational consequences of contract decisions?

### V5 one-line definition

**Oblixa V5 is the contract control system that coordinates decisions, campaigns, and change across a portfolio of signed contracts.**

---

## 2. What V5 should accomplish

V5 should make Oblixa useful not only for executing known work, but for controlling and changing the portfolio when the world moves.

Examples:
- a pricing policy changes,
- a renewal strategy changes,
- a compliance requirement changes,
- a customer requests an amendment,
- ownership shifts between teams,
- a counterparty has to deliver proof or respond,
- a manager needs to understand which decisions matter this month.

The system should move from:
- “we can run the work,”
to
- “we can coordinate, simulate, decide, and document change across many contracts.”

---

## 3. Strategic thesis

### Core thesis

V5 turns Oblixa from an execution platform into a **portfolio control plane** for post-signature contract operations.

### Why this is the right next step

V4 already provides:
- structured contract records,
- generated work objects,
- dependencies,
- evidence collection,
- exceptions,
- reporting,
- policy and SLA enforcement.

The next layer of value is not more isolated objects. It is **cross-contract coordination** and **decision support**.

Professionals do not only need a system that says:
- “here is a task,”
- “here is an obligation,”
- “here is an overdue approval.”

They also need a system that can say:
- “these 38 contracts are affected by the same policy change,”
- “these 12 renewals need a finance + legal + ops decision in the next 30 days,”
- “this exception pattern is spreading across one business segment,”
- “if you change this program or SLA, this much work and risk is created.”

That is the real V5 opportunity.

## 4. Product definition

## What Oblixa V5 is

Oblixa V5 is a platform for:
- coordinating portfolio-level contract change,
- running structured decision workflows,
- managing internal and limited external collaboration,
- forecasting and simulating operational consequences,
- tracking campaigns that span many contracts,
- preserving evidence and rationale behind changes.

## What Oblixa V5 is not

V5 should still avoid becoming:
- a full pre-signature CLM suite,
- a redlining editor,
- a native e-signature platform,
- a generic project management system,
- a vendor-spend/procurement suite,
- a general-purpose BI tool,
- an ungrounded AI chat interface.

The product should stay anchored to signed-contract execution and control.

---

## 5. Product posture after V4

V4 made Oblixa credible as a post-signature execution platform.

V5 should make it indispensable by extending the product into three higher-order jobs:

1. **Decision orchestration**  
   Structured, multi-stakeholder handling of amendments, renewals, waivers, exceptions, and portfolio changes.

2. **Portfolio change management**  
   Controlled rollouts across many contracts, programs, teams, or counterparties.

3. **Operational intelligence**  
   A grounded understanding of upcoming work, risk, capacity, trend lines, and likely bottlenecks.

## 6. V5 product pillars

## Pillar A: Decision Workspaces
Introduce structured workspaces for renewal, amendment, exception, and policy-impact decisions.

## Pillar B: Portfolio Campaigns
Support controlled rollouts and remediation efforts across many contracts at once.

## Pillar C: External Action Layer
Enable secure, bounded collaboration with counterparties and outside participants where needed.

## Pillar D: Operational Intelligence
Surface predictive, portfolio-level, role-aware signals grounded in the actual execution system.

## Pillar E: Account and Counterparty Views
Organize work not only by contract, but by relationship and business context.

## Pillar F: Change Simulation
Let teams preview workload, risk, and policy effects before making operational changes.

---

## 7. V5 design principles

### 1. Decisions deserve structure
High-stakes operational choices should not live in scattered notes and email threads.

### 2. Change should be governable
When dozens of contracts are affected by one policy or event, the system should handle that explicitly.

### 3. Intelligence must be grounded
Every summary, risk callout, forecast, and recommendation must tie back to structured data, event history, and evidence.

### 4. External collaboration should be narrow and controlled
The system may involve outside parties, but it should not become a full external portal suite.

### 5. Portfolio context matters
A contract is important not only by itself, but as part of an account, team, segment, or policy group.

### 6. Explainability beats magic
Users should always be able to answer:
- why did this happen,
- why is this at risk,
- why was this recommended,
- what changes if we act.

## 8. Core V5 product move

### V5 headline move

Introduce **Decision + Campaign orchestration** on top of the V4 execution platform.

This is the most important distinction between V4 and V5.

V4 makes contracts executable.  
V5 makes contract operations **coordinated at scale**.

## 9. Core V5 feature set

## 9.1 Decision Workspaces

### Summary
Create structured decision workspaces for the highest-value contract operations decisions.

### Supported decision types
- renewal recommendation
- amendment request
- waiver or exception disposition
- obligation extension
- ownership transfer decision
- policy exception decision
- termination recommendation
- remediation acceptance decision

### Workspace contents
Each decision workspace should include:
- decision type and status,
- linked contract(s),
- linked account/counterparty,
- decision owner,
- stakeholders,
- due date / SLA,
- required inputs,
- linked tasks/approvals/evidence,
- recommendation,
- decision rationale,
- approval path,
- final disposition,
- post-decision actions generated.

### Why it matters
Many professional workflows are not just work execution. They are decisions with consequences.
V5 should treat that as a first-class product concept.

### Example
A renewal workspace may include:
- pricing recommendation,
- account health notes,
- open obligations,
- outstanding exceptions,
- contract program status,
- legal review status,
- finance sign-off,
- approved outcome,
- generated follow-up plan.

---

## 9.2 Portfolio Campaigns

### Summary
Introduce campaigns as a way to coordinate changes, remediations, and reviews across many contracts.

### Campaign types
- policy rollout
- compliance remediation
- amendment campaign
- data quality campaign
- owner reassignment campaign
- renewal preparation campaign
- evidence collection campaign
- counterparty outreach campaign
- SLA remediation campaign

### Campaign capabilities
- define eligibility rules,
- preview affected contracts,
- assign campaign owner,
- assign work by queue or team,
- create linked work items,
- track progress by segment,
- pause/resume campaign,
- rollback where safe,
- preserve audit trail,
- export campaign status.

### Why this matters
The execution engine is contract-centric.
V5 needs a portfolio-centric operating construct.

### Strategic value
This is one of the clearest steps toward professional indispensability.

---

## 9.3 Account and Counterparty Workspaces

### Summary
Add relationship-centric views above the contract level.

### Workspace contents
For each account or counterparty, show:
- linked contracts,
- active programs,
- open obligations,
- renewal horizon,
- outstanding exceptions,
- approval bottlenecks,
- evidence gaps,
- recent activity,
- campaign membership,
- relationship health signals,
- assigned owners.

### Why it matters
Professionals often manage obligations, renewals, and amendments at the relationship level, not only by one contract at a time.

### Key capability
Support account-level decisions and summaries while preserving contract-level traceability.

---

## 9.4 Limited External Workspaces

### Summary
Provide bounded external participation without becoming a full counterparty portal.

### Supported external actions
- submit evidence,
- acknowledge receipt,
- answer structured request,
- confirm renewal input,
- upload requested document,
- confirm notice delivery,
- respond to amendment intake request,
- complete attestation,
- review exported decision packet.

### Security model
- tokenized and time-bounded access,
- scope-limited actions,
- no broad repository access,
- event logging for every external action,
- optional re-auth or passcode layer for sensitive flows.

### Why it matters
Some of the most painful post-signature work stalls because the other side or an external stakeholder must act.
V5 should support that directly, but in a controlled way.

---

## 9.5 Operational Intelligence Layer

### Summary
Build a grounded intelligence layer on top of execution data.

### Output types
- portfolio risk summaries,
- likely SLA breach forecasts,
- upcoming workload spikes,
- renewal readiness forecasts,
- stale-exception trend analysis,
- missing-evidence risk forecasts,
- campaign completion forecasts,
- bottleneck identification,
- program effectiveness insights,
- decision queue summaries.

### Principles
- no freeform hallucinated advisory output,
- all intelligence tied to structured signals,
- explainable drivers,
- link back to underlying objects.

### Example insights
- “12 finance approvals are likely to breach SLA in the next 5 business days.”
- “Renewal preparation capacity is insufficient for the 30-day horizon in the East region.”
- “This new policy would create 143 tasks and 28 evidence requests across active enterprise agreements.”
- “Three counterparties account for 41% of open obligation risk.”

## 9.6 Change Simulation Studio

### Summary
Expand policy/program simulation into a general change simulation surface.

### Supported simulations
- contract program update impact,
- approval SLA change impact,
- evidence requirement rollout impact,
- escalation policy change impact,
- renewal playbook change impact,
- routing policy change impact,
- campaign eligibility impact,
- new reporting cadence impact.

### Output metrics
- affected contracts,
- generated tasks/approvals/obligations,
- estimated owner load,
- expected escalation increase/decrease,
- segment-specific impact,
- likely backlog created,
- timing distribution.

### Why it matters
V4 can execute changes. V5 should help teams decide whether those changes are wise before rollout.

## 9.8 Workload and Capacity Planning

### Summary
Introduce capacity-aware operational planning.

### Capabilities
- forecast upcoming work by team/role,
- measure load by contract program,
- identify concentration risk,
- show overloaded approvers,
- show under-owned segments,
- compare expected vs actual throughput,
- support redistribution planning.

### Why it matters
A system becomes more valuable when it does not only track work, but helps teams plan whether they can actually complete it.

---

## 9.9 Recommendation Engine (grounded, not generative-first)

### Summary
Add a rules + signal driven recommendation layer for specific operational decisions.

### Recommendation examples
- assign owner suggestion,
- escalation route suggestion,
- campaign segmentation suggestion,
- renewal readiness recommendation,
- evidence requirement suggestion,
- review priority suggestion,
- exception severity suggestion.

### Guardrails
- no autonomous legal advice,
- no opaque scoring without explanation,
- all recommendations reviewable,
- all recommendations traceable to signals and history.

## 10. New major workflows

## Workflow 1: Policy rollout campaign
1. Admin or ops manager defines a new policy or program update.
2. Change Simulation Studio previews affected contracts and expected work.
3. Campaign is created from the simulation result.
4. Work items, evidence requests, or exceptions are generated.
5. Teams execute by queue or region.
6. Progress and breach risk are tracked.
7. Report packs summarize campaign status.

## Workflow 2: Renewal decision orchestration
1. Renewal horizon threshold is reached.
2. Renewal workspace opens.
3. Account-level context and open exceptions are pulled in.
4. Finance, legal, and ops stakeholders contribute.
5. Recommendation is generated and reviewed.
6. Final decision is recorded.
7. Follow-up work and communications are generated.

## Workflow 3: External evidence collection
1. Obligation or campaign requires outside input.
2. Secure external request link is generated.
3. External party submits evidence or structured response.
4. Reviewer validates submission.
5. Missing or rejected submission escalates.
6. All actions are recorded in the casefile and timeline.

## Workflow 4: Capacity-aware approval control
1. System forecasts an SLA risk spike.
2. Manager sees overloaded approvers.
3. Delegation or reassignment plan is proposed.
4. Policy or routing adjustment is simulated.
5. Approved changes are applied.
6. Subsequent metrics compare projected vs actual outcome.

## Workflow 5: Amendment decision workflow
1. Amendment need or request is created.
2. Workspace gathers context, linked obligations, and downstream risk.
3. Stakeholders contribute comments, evidence, and approvals.
4. Packet is generated for decision.
5. Final disposition creates new work, updates program assignments, and preserves rationale.

## 19. Risks and failure modes

### Risk 1: Becoming too abstract
If V5 becomes mostly analytics and recommendations, users may not feel day-to-day value.  
**Mitigation:** keep decisions and campaigns tightly connected to operational work.

### Risk 2: Overcomplicating external collaboration
A full external portal would add major scope and support burden.  
**Mitigation:** keep external participation narrow, tokenized, and workflow-specific.

### Risk 3: Weak simulation credibility
If simulation outputs are too rough, users will distrust them.  
**Mitigation:** keep simulation based on actual program logic and historical workload.

### Risk 4: Excessive IA sprawl
More power can make navigation worse.  
**Mitigation:** consolidate around control loops: work, decisions, campaigns, reports.

### Risk 5: Recommendation overreach
Unclear or overly “AI” recommendations will weaken trust.  
**Mitigation:** prefer signal-based recommendations with explicit reasons and evidence.

### Risk 6: Losing the sharp market position
Moving too far into general account management or BI would blur the product.  
**Mitigation:** keep all new concepts anchored to signed-contract operations.

## 21. Final definition

**Oblixa V5 is the version where the platform stops being only an execution engine and becomes a contract control system for coordinated change.**

It should help professionals:
- run portfolio-wide campaigns,
- orchestrate high-stakes decisions,
- involve the right internal and external participants,
- simulate the consequences of changes,
- understand risk and capacity,

If V4 proved that Oblixa can execute contract work, V5 should prove that it can coordinate contract operations at scale.

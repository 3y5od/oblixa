## 1. Overview

Oblixa V4 should not be a broader generic CLM suite.

It should become a **contract execution system**: the operating layer teams use after signature to turn contracts into governed work, accountable ownership, evidence, escalation, and reporting.

V1 established the contract record, extraction, review, and reminder model.  
V2 expanded the system into multi-team contract operations with tasks, approvals, obligations, renewals, reporting, saved views, imports/exports, integrations, and automation.  
V3 solidified the platform posture with role-based capabilities, feature-flagged workflow modules, webhook/event infrastructure, rate limiting, RLS-backed tenancy, scheduled automation, observability, and stronger testing/release gates.

V4 should unify these capabilities into a more opinionated and professionally valuable system that answers one central question:

**Once a contract is signed, how does the work actually get done, proven, escalated, and audited?**

## 3. V4 Thesis

### Core thesis
V4 turns Oblixa from a contract operations platform into a **contract execution operating system**.

### Practical meaning
Instead of only storing records and exposing modules, V4 should:
- automatically generate work from contract data and workflow policy,
- route work to the correct owners,
- track whether obligations were actually fulfilled,
- escalate risk when tasks or approvals stall,
- preserve operational evidence,
- expose role-specific views of what matters now,
- provide managers with a live picture of execution health.

### Why this is the right next move
The current platform already has most of the necessary primitives:
- contract records and files,
- AI extraction and review,
- tasks and automation,
- approvals,
- obligations,
- renewals,
- watchlists,
- saved views,
- reports,
- imports and exports,
- calendar feeds,
- integrations,
- outbound events and webhooks,
- role/capability controls,
- tenant isolation,
- scheduled jobs and reliability mechanisms.

V4 should not add breadth for its own sake. It should make the existing system cohere into a product professionals depend on every week.

## 4. Product Definition

## What Oblixa is
Oblixa is a system for running post-signature contract work:
- obligation execution,
- renewal preparation,
- approval handling,
- exception management,
- accountability tracking,
- evidence collection,
- operational reporting.

## What Oblixa is not
V4 should explicitly avoid becoming:
- a drafting editor,
- a redlining/negotiation platform,
- a native e-signature suite,
- a broad procurement vendor-management platform,
- a general-purpose workflow builder for non-contract use cases,
- an AI chat product without operational consequence.

The product should remain tightly focused on post-signature execution.

## 6. Strategic Objectives for V4

### Objective 1: Make signed contracts executable
Every important contractual commitment should be convertible into structured operational work.

### Objective 2: Make ownership explicit
Every task, obligation, approval, renewal checkpoint, and exception should have a current owner, due context, escalation path, and visible status.

### Objective 3: Make evidence first-class
Teams should be able to prove that work happened, not just claim it happened.

### Objective 4: Make risk governable
Exceptions, stale work, missing data, and policy violations should surface in a unified operational control layer.

### Objective 5: Make the system manager-useful
Managers should be able to understand workload, execution health, bottlenecks, SLA performance, and upcoming decisions without assembling reports manually.

### Objective 6: Increase daily usefulness
The product should become part of recurring weekly and monthly operational workflows, not only a repository consulted occasionally.

---

## 7. Product Principles

### 1. Action over storage
The system should prioritize what must be done, by whom, by when, and why.

### 2. Trust through evidence
Users should always be able to trace tasks, dates, obligations, and decisions back to sources, policies, and event history.

### 3. Explicit workflows beat flexible ambiguity
The product should remain opinionated where opinionation improves clarity, consistency, and operational value.

### 4. Exceptions deserve first-class treatment
Breakdowns and blockers should be visible, routable, and measurable.

### 5. Role relevance matters
Different professional users need different command surfaces and decision cues.

### 6. Automation should reduce coordination load
Automation should route work and generate visibility, not create opaque background behavior.

## 9. Core V4 Features

## 9.1 Contract Programs

### Summary
Introduce **Contract Programs** as the central execution template layer for recurring contract workflows.

A Contract Program is a reusable operational blueprint that can be attached automatically or manually to contracts based on type, source, counterparty, or policy.

### Contract Program contents
A program may define:
- task bundles,
- obligation bundles,
- approval sequences,
- renewal checkpoint sequences,
- SLAs,
- escalation rules,
- evidence requirements,
- report subscriptions,
- role routing defaults.

### Why this matters
Today the system contains task templates, obligation templates, reminder templates, renewal playbook templates, and approval policies. V4 should unify those into a more legible and manageable concept.

### Example
A "Customer MSA Program" could automatically generate:
- a 90-day renewal checkpoint,
- a finance review checkpoint,
- quarterly obligation attestations,
- annual pricing review task,
- approval path for amendments,
- escalation to ops manager after missed obligation SLA.

### Requirements
- Program catalog page
- Program versioning
- Draft/published states
- Impact preview before applying updates
- Auto-assignment rules
- Contract-level override support
- Program usage analytics

## 9.2 Execution Graph

### Summary
Add an execution graph model that links work items across tasks, approvals, obligations, and renewals.

### Goal
Users should be able to understand:
- what work was generated,
- what depends on what,
- which item is blocking progress,
- what happens next if a given item completes or fails.

### Supported relationships
- task depends on approval
- approval blocked by missing field/review
- obligation completion requires evidence
- renewal checkpoint depends on task completion
- exception linked to one or more work items

### UX outcomes
- visual dependency display
- “blocked by” and “unblocks” chips
- execution timeline per contract
- portfolio-level blocker summary

---

## 9.3 Exception Ledger

### Summary
Create a dedicated exception system that governs operational issues rather than merely displaying them.

### Exception types
- overdue task
- overdue obligation
- approval SLA breach
- missing critical field
- missing owner
- renewal decision at risk
- policy mismatch
- failed integration sync
- repeated delivery failure
- stale contract review cadence
- data quality anomaly

### Exception behaviors
Each exception should support:
- severity,
- owner,
- due date,
- status,
- root cause,
- linked artifacts,
- linked contract/program/work objects,
- escalation history,
- resolution note,
- reopen behavior.

### Why it matters
Professionals trust systems that handle failure modes explicitly.
V4 should make exceptions a core operating layer.

## 10. Detailed Workflow Targets

## Workflow 1: New contract becomes executable
1. Contract is uploaded or imported.
2. Extraction runs.
3. Review validates critical fields.
4. Contract Program auto-attaches.
5. Obligations/tasks/approvals/checkpoints are generated.
6. Owners are assigned by policy.
7. Dashboard and command centers update.
8. Scheduled reports and external sync activate.

## Workflow 2: Obligation fulfillment with proof
1. Obligation becomes due.
2. Owner receives notification.
3. Owner submits evidence or attestation.
4. Reviewer approves or rejects.
5. If stale, SLA breach creates exception.
6. Manager sees risk on command center.
7. Evidence becomes part of casefile and report packs.

## Workflow 3: Renewal decision management
1. Renewal horizon threshold is hit.
2. Renewal workspace opens from template.
3. Tasks and stakeholders are routed.
4. Inputs are collected.
5. Decision gate is reached.
6. Renewal recommendation is approved or escalated.
7. Outcome syncs to downstream systems.
8. History and lessons remain on contract casefile.

## Workflow 4: Approval bottleneck escalation
1. Approval is requested.
2. SLA countdown begins.
3. Approver receives reminder.
4. Breach threshold creates exception.
5. Escalation routes to manager/delegate.
6. Timeline and metrics capture delay.
7. Report packs include recurring bottleneck pattern.

## Workflow 5: Data-quality remediation campaign
1. System detects missing critical fields / stale reviews / invalid ownership.
2. Campaign is created in Maintenance Studio.
3. Contracts are grouped into a remediation queue.
4. Batch review and assignment are completed.
5. Progress is measured until queue resolves.

## 18. Risks and Failure Modes

### Risk 1: Overbuilding workflow complexity
Mitigation: remain focused on contract execution, not generic workflow software.

### Risk 2: Excessive configuration burden
Mitigation: ship strong defaults and template packs before deep customization.

### Risk 3: Hidden automation confusion
Mitigation: every automated action must have visible provenance and event history.

### Risk 4: Fragmented IA
Mitigation: consolidate around execution concepts rather than proliferating side modules.

### Risk 5: Weak evidence usability
Mitigation: make evidence submission lightweight and role-appropriate.

### Risk 6: Trying to compete directly with end-to-end CLM suites
Mitigation: maintain sharp positioning around post-signature execution.

## 19. Non-Goals

V4 should not prioritize:
- native drafting editor
- Microsoft Word redlining parity
- clause library authoring
- in-product negotiation workspace
- native e-signature stack
- procurement / spend / vendor onboarding platform breadth
- general AI assistant unrelated to concrete workflows

## 20. Final Definition

**Oblixa V4 is the version where a signed contract becomes an executable operational system.**

It should generate work, route ownership, enforce timelines, collect evidence, escalate issues, and expose risk and progress to the people responsible for outcomes.

That is the clearest path to making the product genuinely useful and highly valuable to professionals.

If V3 proved that the platform can support real workflows, V4 should prove that professionals can rely on Oblixa to run them.
